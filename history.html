Hidden no more

Women Who Built Tech

The history of computing is full of women who were overlooked, uncredited, and forgotten. Not anymore.

The pioneers

Six women who changed everything.

Ada Lovelace

1815 – 1852

Often called the world's first computer programmer, Ada wrote the first algorithm intended to be processed by a machine — nearly 100 years before the first computer was built.

Programming

Grace Hopper

1906 – 1992

Rear Admiral Grace Hopper invented the first compiler and helped develop COBOL, one of the earliest programming languages. She also popularized the term "debugging."

Compilers & Languages

Katherine Johnson

1918 – 2020

A NASA mathematician whose orbital mechanics calculations were critical to the success of the first U.S. crewed spaceflights. Her story was told in the film Hidden Figures.

Space Computing

Dorothy Vaughan

1910 – 2008

NASA's first Black supervisor, Dorothy taught herself and her team FORTRAN when electronic computers arrived — ensuring no one on her team was left behind by the technology shift.

Leadership & FORTRAN

Radia Perlman

1951 – present

Known as the "Mother of the Internet," Radia invented the Spanning Tree Protocol — a fundamental algorithm that makes modern internet networking possible.

Networking

Hedy Lamarr

1914 – 2000

Famous as a Hollywood actress, Hedy co-invented frequency-hopping spread spectrum during WWII — technology that laid the groundwork for WiFi, GPS, and Bluetooth.

Wireless Tech
Featured story

The ENIAC Programmers — the women history forgot.

In 1945, six women — Kay McNulty, Betty Jennings, Betty Snyder, Marlyn Wescoff, Fran Bilas, and Ruth Lichterman — were selected to program ENIAC, one of the world's first general-purpose electronic computers.

They received no formal training, no manuals, and no recognition. Working only from circuit diagrams, they figured out how to program the machine entirely on their own. Their work helped calculate ballistic trajectories for the U.S. Army during World War II.

When ENIAC was unveiled to the public in 1946, the six women were not introduced. They were not in the press photos. They were not mentioned. For decades, their contributions were almost entirely erased from history.

Today, thanks to the work of historian Kathy Kleiman and the documentary Top Secret Rosies, their story is finally being told.

Key moments

A brief timeline of women in computing.

  • 1843
    Ada Lovelace publishes the first algorithm Written for Charles Babbage's Analytical Engine — a machine that was never built in her lifetime.
  • 1944
    Grace Hopper joins the Harvard Mark I team One of the first programmers on the machine, she later invented the first compiler.
  • 1945
    The ENIAC Six program the world's first computer Six women program ENIAC with no manual, no training, and no credit.
  • 1958
    Dorothy Vaughan leads NASA's computing pool Becomes NASA's first Black supervisor and leads the transition to electronic computing.
  • 1962
    Katherine Johnson calculates John Glenn's orbit Glenn insisted Johnson verify the electronic computer's numbers before he would fly.
  • 1985
    Radia Perlman invents the Spanning Tree Protocol Her algorithm becomes the backbone of modern internet networking.
  • Today
    Women continue to shape the future of tech From AI research to cybersecurity to UX design — the work continues.
